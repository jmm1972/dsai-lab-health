About the Hub

Research at the interface of AI and medicine

The DSAI Hub for Health brings together two complementary principal investigators — combining expertise in advanced statistical and AI methodology with deep clinical knowledge in paediatric cardiology, autonomic physiology, and electrophysiology. Our research is organised around four pillars: trustworthy and explainable AI, clinical AI translation, precision cardiology, and causal & computational statistics. We partner with clinicians, health organisations, and technology developers to produce research that is technically excellent and genuinely useful in healthcare settings.

Trustworthy AI

Explainability, robustness, fairness, causal reasoning, and privacy-preserving learning in clinical contexts.

Clinical AI Translation

Adoption science, organisational readiness, digital transformation, and regulatory pathways for AI as a medical device.

Precision Cardiology

Digital twins, wearable foundation models, inherited arrhythmias, paediatric CHD, and continuous risk surveillance.

Causal & Computational Statistics

Causal inference, survival analysis, high-dimensional modelling, synthetic data, and spatiotemporal methods for health research.

PhD
SHIFT-XAI
Explainability Under Distribution Shift in Medical Imaging AI
Investigating the robustness of explainability methods under real-world data shift in medical imaging, establishing benchmarks and methods for cross-site deployment.
Read full description ▼

Medical imaging has become one of the most active areas for AI development in healthcare. Yet, concerns remain about generalisability across hospitals, scanners, protocols, and patient populations. These concerns extend beyond predictive performance to the explanations used to justify model outputs. Explanation methods may appear plausible but become unstable or misleading under distribution shifts, creating a serious barrier to safe translation into clinical practice.

This project will investigate the robustness of explainability methods under real-world data shift in medical imaging. It will establish benchmark settings for stress-testing explanations, compare intrinsically interpretable and post hoc approaches, and develop methods to detect and mitigate degradation in explanation quality across settings. The project will generate high-impact methodological and practical outputs, including evaluation protocols for robust explainability, guidance for cross-site deployment, and evidence to support more reliable use of imaging AI in heterogeneous clinical environments.

PhD
READY-AI
Organisational Readiness and Digital Maturity for AI Adoption in Hospitals
Designing and validating multidimensional frameworks for assessing institutional preparedness for AI adoption — from governance and data quality to staff capability.
Read full description ▼

The successful adoption of AI in hospitals depends not only on the quality of the technology but on the institution's capacity to integrate it responsibly into clinical and organisational practice. Hospitals differ substantially in their digital maturity, governance structures, interoperability, data quality, staff capabilities, and readiness to evaluate and sustain innovation. Yet there is still no widely accepted framework for measuring institutional preparedness for AI adoption in a rigorous, comparable, and operationally useful way.

This project will design and validate a multidimensional framework for assessing organisational readiness and digital maturity for AI adoption in hospital settings. It will develop and test measurement instruments across institutions with varying levels of digital capability, generating evidence that can support benchmarking, targeted improvement, and more effective implementation strategies.

PhD
PaedECG-FM
A Foundation Model for the Developing Heart
Designing the first paediatric-aware ECG foundation model using age-conditioned self-supervision, structural tokenisation, and few-shot adaptation for rare arrhythmia phenotypes.
Read full description ▼

Paediatric electrocardiography sits at the intersection of two challenges that current AI cannot solve. ECG morphology evolves continuously with age, body habitus, and cardiac structural maturation, violating the implicit stationarity assumption of adult-trained models. At the same time, paediatric arrhythmias and channelopathies are individually rare and labelled data is scarce and fragmented across small specialist centres.

This project will design and pretrain the first paediatric-aware ECG foundation model, combining age-conditioned self-supervision, structural-heterogeneity-aware tokenisation, and few-shot adaptation strategies for rare phenotypes. The work will draw on a large multicentre paediatric ECG resource and external validation through ERN GUARD-Heart. Expected outcomes include open-source pretrained weights, a reusable paediatric ECG benchmark suite, and demonstrated clinical utility for problems such as Long-QT misclassification, post-operative arrhythmia detection in CHD, and Brugada phenotype recognition.

PhD
MNAR-TIME
Causal Inference with Time-Varying Treatments and Informative Missingness in Longitudinal Clinical Data
Developing unified methodology for causal inference with time-varying treatments and non-ignorable missing data — addressing a critical gap in pharmacoepidemiology and comparative effectiveness research.
Read full description ▼

Estimating the causal effects of treatments that change over time is a central challenge in pharmacoepidemiology and comparative effectiveness research. Methods such as marginal structural models, g-estimation, and targeted learning have established a rigorous theoretical foundation, but nearly all assume that the mechanism generating missing data is ignorable given observed history. In practice, clinical records are replete with informative dropout.

This project will develop a unified methodological framework for causal inference with time-varying treatments in the presence of non-ignorable missingness in longitudinal observational data. It will formalise a class of shared-parameter semi-MNAR models that couple the dropout mechanism directly with the causal model, and derive identification conditions under which the estimands of interest remain recoverable. Estimation will proceed within a Bayesian hierarchical framework, enabling principled uncertainty propagation across both the causal and missingness components.

PhD
FairFed-Health
Federated Learning with Statistical Fairness Guarantees under Non-IID Heterogeneity in Healthcare
Building theoretically grounded federated learning frameworks with explicit statistical guarantees on model quality and algorithmic fairness under realistic non-IID conditions across healthcare institutions.
Read full description ▼

Federated learning has become an attractive paradigm for collaborative model development across healthcare institutions, allowing sites to contribute to a shared model without transferring sensitive patient data. Yet, the settings in which federated learning is most valuable are also those in which it faces the most serious statistical difficulties. Data distributions across institutions are rarely exchangeable, violating the IID assumption that underpins most aggregation strategies.

This project will develop a theoretically grounded framework for federated learning in healthcare that provides explicit statistical guarantees on both model quality and algorithmic fairness under realistic non-IID conditions. It will formalise fairness criteria that are estimable without access to raw data, characterise the asymptotic tradeoff between global efficiency and site-level equity, and propose weighted aggregation schemes with coverage guarantees under distributional shift. Expected outputs include a formal theory of federated fairness, open-source methodology, and evidence relevant to regulatory frameworks governing AI in healthcare.

PhD
ADOPT-AI
Modelling the Adoption of Clinical AI Beyond Intention to Use
Building empirical frameworks for AI adoption in healthcare, moving beyond self-reported intentions to capture real organisational, behavioural, and institutional mechanisms.
Read full description ▼

Healthcare organisations are investing in artificial intelligence at an accelerating pace. Yet, many systems do not progress beyond pilot implementation or fail to become embedded in routine care. Existing research on adoption often relies too heavily on perceived usefulness or intention to use, while paying insufficient attention to observed behaviour, organisational conditions, workflow integration, and institutional context.

This project will develop a new empirical framework for understanding the adoption of AI-based tools in healthcare by combining established implementation models with real-world data on use, uptake, and organisational readiness. It will move beyond self-reported intentions and capture the mechanisms by which AI systems are accepted, resisted, adapted to, or abandoned in practice. Expected outcomes include a more realistic, analytically grounded model of AI adoption, validated metrics linking intention and actual use, and practical insights to support the design and implementation of digital technologies with a higher likelihood of sustained impact.

PhD
WearSSL
Self-Supervised Foundation Models for Continuous Cardiovascular Risk
Pretraining multimodal self-supervised foundation models on heterogeneous wearable data for joint circadian, autonomic, and arrhythmic risk prediction in free-living settings.
Read full description ▼

Consumer-grade wearables now generate orders of magnitude more cardiovascular time series than hospital monitoring systems, but their scientific exploitation remains shallow. Most published work either fine-tunes narrow classifiers on single tasks or repurposes architectures designed for hospital ECG, ignoring the unique noise structure, intermittency, and heterogeneity of free-living signals.

This project will pretrain a multimodal self-supervised foundation model on heterogeneous wearable data, including PPG, single-lead ECG, accelerometry, and sleep stages, using masked modelling and contrastive objectives designed to be robust to modality dropout and irregular sampling. The model will be evaluated on pre-syncopal state detection, post-Fontan early decompensation, and arrhythmia prediction in inherited channelopathies. Expected outputs include an open-pretrained backbone, an evaluation suite spanning paediatric and adult cardiology, and clinical evidence of incremental value over current single-task baselines.

Postdoc
CAUSE-DIGI
Causal Evaluation of Digital Technology Adoption and Workflow Impact in Healthcare
Applying causal inference and quasi-experimental methods to evaluate the real-world impact of AI-enabled technologies on clinical workflow, organisational performance, and service delivery.
Read full description ▼

Digital technologies are frequently introduced into healthcare with the promise of improving efficiency, quality, and decision-making. Yet, the evidence based on their real-world effects is often weak. Adoption studies commonly focus on user perceptions or simple before-and-after comparisons, which are not sufficient to determine whether a technology genuinely improves workflow, reduces burden, or changes patient pathways in meaningful ways.

This project will apply causal inference and quasi-experimental methods to evaluate the impact of digital and AI-enabled technologies on clinical workflow, organisational performance, and service delivery. By integrating routinely collected operational data with implementation indicators, the project will identify what changes after deployment, for whom, and under what conditions.

Postdoc
RECOVER-SYNCOPE
From a Tilt-Table Cohort to a European Decision-Support Tool
Transforming an existing tilt-table cohort into a validated, deployable decision-support tool for syncope triage, with external validation across European syncope centres.
Read full description ▼

Syncope is among the most common reasons for unscheduled cardiology evaluation. Yet, diagnostic pathways remain fragmented and tilt-table testing — the established mechanistic gold standard — is interpreted with substantial centre-level variability. Years of structured tilt-table data accumulated within a single high-volume syncope unit constitute a methodologically rich yet underexploited resource.

This project will transform an existing tilt-table cohort into a clinically useful decision-support tool for syncope, combining interpretable autonomic-signal modelling with phenotype clustering anchored on clinical outcomes rather than tilt response alone. The work will pre-register an external validation study across at least two additional European syncope centres and integrate the model into a clinically governed prototype within the host hospital information system.

Postdoc
SynthStat
Statistical Properties of Synthetic Health Data: Estimand Preservation, Causal Fidelity, and Regulatory Validity
Establishing formal statistical theory of estimand preservation for synthetic health data — characterising when marginal, associational, and causal estimands converge to their population counterparts under deep generative models.
Read full description ▼

The generation of synthetic health data using deep generative models — variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, and diffusion models — is rapidly expanding in scope and ambition, driven by the prospect of enabling secondary analysis, collaborative research, and regulatory submissions without exposing individual-level records. Despite this momentum, the statistical foundations of synthetic data for inference remain underdeveloped.

This project will establish a formal statistical theory of estimand preservation for synthetic health data, characterising the conditions under which marginal, associational, and causal estimands derived from synthetic data converge to their counterparts in the original population. It will develop diagnostic tests for causal fidelity, propose metrics for regulatory use grounded in statistical decision theory, and formally analyse the privacy-utility frontier. The framework will be applied to representative clinical datasets with direct relevance to the European Health Data Space and the AI Act.

Postdoc
MultiLat-SEM
Unified Latent Factor Models for Multimodal Longitudinal Clinical Data with Complex Missingness
Developing Bayesian latent factor frameworks for multimodal longitudinal data combining imaging biomarkers, laboratory measurements, patient-reported outcomes, and genomic information with modality-specific missingness mechanisms.
Read full description ▼

Clinical research increasingly draws on data from multiple sources simultaneously, combining imaging biomarkers, laboratory measurements, patient-reported outcome scales, and genomic information within a single cohort. These modalities are heterogeneous in measurement scale, temporal resolution, and missingness pattern, and they are conceptually linked through shared latent constructs that no single modality measures directly.

This project will develop a unified Bayesian latent factor framework for multimodal longitudinal data that accommodates heterogeneous measurement scales, modality-specific missingness mechanisms, and time-varying factor structure within a single coherent model. Expected deliverables include a formal identifiability analysis, scalable open-source software, and applied evidence from multimodal clinical studies.

Postdoc
CalibDrift
Prospective Calibration Monitoring and Recalibration of Clinical Prediction Models under Concept Drift
Developing sequential testing and conformal prediction frameworks for prospective calibration monitoring of deployed clinical prediction models — providing finite-sample error rate control and adaptive recalibration.
Read full description ▼

Clinical prediction models are developed and validated in one population and subsequently deployed in settings that may differ substantially from the development context. Coding practices change, demographic profiles shift, treatment guidelines evolve, and new comorbidities emerge over time. Current approaches to recalibration are largely retrospective and do not provide statistical guarantees on coverage or error control under sequential inference.

This project will develop a framework for prospective calibration monitoring and adaptive recalibration of clinical prediction models under concept drift, drawing on sequential testing, online learning, and conformal prediction. The framework will be applied to prediction models deployed in multi-year electronic health record environments in oncology, cardiology, and emergency medicine.

Postdoc
VAL2USE-AI
From Validation to Uptake: Adoption Pathways for AI and Software as a Medical Device
Characterising and modelling how AI and software as a medical device move from technical validation to sustained clinical adoption — identifying conditions that support or block translation.
Read full description ▼

Many AI tools in healthcare achieve encouraging performance in development studies but fail to reach sustained use in practice. This translational gap reflects the fact that adoption is shaped by more than algorithmic accuracy. Interoperability, regulation, procurement, workflow fit, user trust, explainability, training, and post-deployment monitoring all influence whether a tool progresses from technical validation to meaningful integration.

This project will characterise and model the pathway through which AI and software as a medical device move from validation to uptake in real healthcare systems. Expected outputs include an integrated translational framework, evidence-informed recommendations for developers and health organisations, and a stronger basis for designing adoption strategies that are realistic, scalable, and aligned with healthcare needs.

Postdoc
PEDIA-PASS
Continuous Outpatient Surveillance after Fontan Palliation
Developing a clinically governed continuous surveillance pathway for Fontan patients using multimodal wearables, home spirometry, and patient-reported outcomes with uncertainty quantification.
Read full description ▼

Patients living with single-ventricle physiology after Fontan palliation now constitute a growing adult congenital heart disease population in whom subclinical decompensation typically precedes catastrophic events by weeks to months. Their care is currently structured around in-clinic follow-up at intervals that are demonstrably too coarse to detect early haemodynamic, autonomic, and arrhythmic deterioration.

This project will develop and prospectively evaluate a continuous outpatient surveillance pathway for Fontan patients, combining multimodal wearable signals, home spirometry, and longitudinal patient-reported outcomes within a clinically governed monitoring platform. Expected outcomes include a validated digital surveillance pathway, evidence on event-prediction lead time, and a deployment-ready protocol suitable for replication across European adult-CHD centres.
